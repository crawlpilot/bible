# Running Effective 1:1s

**Category:** Manager Frameworks · Coaching · Relationship Building  
**Framework:** Career Conversation (Past → Present → Future) · 1:1 Agenda Structure  
**Interview context:** "How do you structure your 1:1s?" / "How do you understand what motivates the engineers you work with?" / "How do you build trust with engineers you don't manage directly?"

> A 1:1 where the manager talks for 40 minutes and the engineer talks for 5 is not a 1:1 — it is a status briefing with an audience of one.

---

## Why 1:1s Are a PE-Level Skill

A principal engineer without direct reports still has 1:1s — with their manager, with tech leads they mentor, with engineers they're developing, with peers on adjacent teams. The 1:1 is the primary context for:

- Building the trust that makes influence possible
- Surfacing problems before they become crises
- Developing engineers' career trajectories over time
- Giving and receiving feedback in a psychologically safe context
- Understanding what motivates and concerns the people you depend on

A principal engineer who has no structured 1:1 practice is flying blind on the human side of their technical organisation. They're solving for code quality without knowing whether the people writing the code are engaged, blocked, or about to leave.

---

## The Purpose of a 1:1

A 1:1 is a **development conversation** — not a status report, not a project update, not an escalation escalation path.

If you find yourself reviewing sprint velocity, bug counts, or ticket status in a 1:1, stop. That information belongs in a standup or async update. The 1:1 is the one meeting where the engineer's growth, concerns, and motivation are the explicit subject. This framing changes everything about how the meeting runs.

**What a 1:1 is for:**
- Understanding the engineer's current state: engaged, frustrated, uncertain, motivated, at risk
- Development conversations: skills, scope, career trajectory
- Feedback in both directions — more safely given in 1:1 than in a group
- Surfacing blockers, concerns, or observations the engineer wouldn't raise in a group

**What a 1:1 is not for:**
- Status updates on projects (async or standup)
- Solving technical problems together (a separate working session)
- Formal performance reviews (a separate cadence with HR structure)
- Announcements that don't require conversation (email)

## Cadence: Weekly vs. Bi-Weekly

| Cadence | When to use | Trade-offs |
|---------|------------|------------|
| **Weekly (30 min)** | New engineers, engineers in a growth phase, engineers you sense are struggling | High trust-building cadence; tighter feedback loops; higher time cost |
| **Bi-weekly (45–60 min)** | Established relationship, high-performing engineer in steady state | More depth per meeting; risk of losing signal between sessions |
| **Ad hoc only** | Never — for engineers you care about developing | Signals low priority; loses the rhythm that makes 1:1s useful |

**The default:** Start weekly with anyone new to the relationship. Move to bi-weekly once you have a stable sense of where they are and the relationship has enough trust to surface concerns quickly.

**The warning sign:** If you skip 1:1s regularly because "nothing major to discuss," you are missing the point. The low-signal meetings maintain the relationship and trust that make the high-signal meetings possible when something does come up. The engineer who has had 4 weeks of good 1:1s will tell you they're thinking about leaving. The engineer whose 1:1s have been cancelled for a month will tell their recruiter first.

## The 1:1 Agenda Structure

**Rule 1: Their topics come first. Always.**

Open with: "What's on your mind?" or "What do you want to cover today?"

This signals whose meeting this is. The engineer should be bringing things to this meeting, not receiving things from you. If they come with nothing — that's useful information too: either everything is genuinely fine, or they don't yet trust the space enough to bring real concerns.

**Rule 2: Have a loose structure, not a script.**

A rigid agenda ("first we cover career, then project, then feedback") turns a conversation into a meeting. A loose structure ("I want to make sure we leave time for the career conversation today") allows the conversation to go where it needs to go while ensuring important topics aren't perpetually crowded out.

**Rule 3: Leave time for what they didn't raise.**

After their topics: "Is there anything else on your mind?" — the real answer often comes 25 minutes in, after rapport is established, not in the first 5.

**A rough template (30-minute weekly 1:1):**

```
0–5 min:    Their agenda — "What's on your mind this week?"
5–20 min:   Their topics (project concerns, blockers, frustrations,
             something they're excited about)
20–25 min:  Your topics (feedback, context they need, a question
             you have for them)
25–30 min:  "Is there anything else?" + close
```

## The Career Conversation

Most 1:1s cover the immediate and tactical. Career development conversations are different — they require a different pace, more intentional questions, and a longer time horizon.

**The Career Conversation framework (Lara Hogan):**

```
PAST:    "Tell me about the times you've been most energised at work.
          What were you doing? What made it energising?"
          
PRESENT: "What are you most proud of right now? What's
          frustrating you?"
          
FUTURE:  "Where do you want to be in 3 years? What kind of work
          do you want to be doing? What do you want to have built
          or learned?"
```

This is not a performance conversation (what are you doing?) or a goal-setting conversation (what should you do next?). It is a *motivation conversation* — understanding what drives this person, what they find meaningful, and what they're trying to build in their career.

**Why this matters for the principal engineer:**  
The answers to these questions tell you where each engineer wants to grow, which kinds of problems will engage them, and what would cause them to leave. That information shapes how you delegate, how you assign stretch work, and how you advocate for them in calibration.

**Scheduling it:**  
Don't try to run the career conversation in a regular 30-minute 1:1. Book a separate 60–90 minute conversation explicitly for this. "I want to understand more about your career goals and make sure the work you're doing is actually moving toward what matters to you. Can we find 90 minutes in the next 2 weeks?"

## Questions That Surface Hidden Blockers

Most engineers underreport problems in 1:1s. They don't want to seem like they're complaining, or they're unsure whether their concern is legitimate, or they're worried about how it will reflect on them. Standard questions ("how are things going?") produce standard answers ("good, pretty busy").

**Better questions:**

*On blockers:*
- "What is the one thing most slowing down your work right now?"
- "If you could change one thing about how the team works, what would it be?"

*On wellbeing:*
- "On a scale of 1–10, how energised are you right now? What would make it higher?" (The number is less important than what they say about it)
- "What's been the most frustrating part of this week?"

*On the team:*
- "Is there anything happening on the team that you think I should know about but might not?"
- "Is there anyone on the team you're finding hard to collaborate with? What's the dynamic?"

*On their growth:*
- "What's something you've been wanting to learn or try that you haven't had the chance to?"
- "Is there a decision you made recently that you're uncertain about? What's the uncertainty?"

These questions work because they're specific enough to produce specific answers, and they normalise that problems and uncertainties are worth naming.

## 1:1s at Principal Level (Without Direct Reports)

The principal engineer's 1:1 relationships are different from a manager's. You don't own performance reviews, salary, or promotions. You do own technical development and influence. This changes the 1:1 dynamic.

**Your 1:1s with the engineers you mentor:**  
These are coaching conversations more than management conversations. The engineer chooses to engage with you because they find it valuable — which means you have to actually make them valuable. The agenda should be heavily weighted toward their topics. Your job is to develop their thinking, not to manage their delivery.

**Your 1:1 with your manager:**  
Often neglected or dominated by project updates. The principal engineer should be using their manager 1:1 for: getting context you need (organisational changes, priorities you're not aware of), giving upward feedback, surfacing risks your manager should know about, and discussing your own development.

The manager 1:1 where you spend 20 minutes on project status and 5 minutes on everything else is a missed opportunity. Flip it: async on status, synchronous on context, strategy, and relationship.

**Skip-level 1:1s (with your manager's manager):**  
Used sparingly. Not for escalating over your manager's head. Used for: building organisational visibility at a level above your manager, understanding the broader strategic context that informs your technical direction, and occasionally for a second perspective on a decision that affects a wide scope. If you're going to build org-wide influence, the relationships two levels above you matter.

## Noticing When a 1:1 Needs to Change

**Signs the 1:1 is not working:**
- Engineer consistently brings nothing to talk about (no trust or no perceived value)
- Conversations are always about project status (not used as development space)
- You're always the one talking (the meeting has become a briefing)
- You haven't learned anything new about the engineer in the last 4 sessions
- You're cancelling it regularly (it's not being prioritised)

**Signs the relationship is in trouble:**
- Engineer's answers are shorter than usual — shorter answers often signal disengagement or a concern they haven't named
- They stop raising problems (they've concluded problems don't get resolved)
- They start being less available for informal conversations

When you notice these signs, name them explicitly: "I've noticed our 1:1s have been feeling more transactional lately. What would make them more useful for you?"

## PE vs. Mid-Level on 1:1s

| Dimension | Principal Engineer | Senior / Staff Engineer |
|-----------|-------------------|------------------------|
| **Agenda control** | Their topics first; principal's topics last | Drives the agenda themselves |
| **Purpose** | Development, trust, signal-gathering | Project updates (often) |
| **Career conversation** | Explicitly scheduled, separate from regular 1:1s | Rarely happens unless engineer initiates |
| **Questions** | Open, specific, designed to surface what isn't said | "How are things going?" |
| **Upward 1:1** | Uses manager 1:1 strategically for context and feedback | Status updates |
| **Skip-levels** | Maintains selectively for org awareness | Rarely uses |
| **Cancellation tolerance** | Protects 1:1 cadence as a priority | Cancels when "nothing urgent to discuss" |
| **Trust-building** | Explicit — takes notes, follows up, closes loops | Incidental |

## Common Interviewer Follow-Up Questions

**"How do you structure your 1:1s with engineers you mentor?"**

> "Their agenda, their time. I open every 1:1 by asking what's on their mind — and I mean it as a genuine invitation, not a formality. The engineers I work most closely with bring real things: a decision they're uncertain about, a conflict they're navigating, something they're excited about that they want to think through. My job is to ask good questions, not to solve their problems for them. I do bring my own topics — usually feedback from something I observed, or context about the team or org that's useful for them to have. But I put my topics last and keep them brief. The developmental piece is in the questions I ask, not in the information I provide. I also try to track what I learn across sessions: what motivates them, what frustrates them, where their confidence gaps are. If I come back three sessions later and reference something they told me in the first one, it signals that I was actually listening — which builds the trust that makes people willing to bring the real stuff."

**"How do you surface what engineers are actually worried about, rather than what they're willing to say?"**

> "Two things: patience and specific questions. Most engineers will tell you the sanitised version in the first 10 minutes — 'things are fine, just busy.' The real answer often comes after you've established that you're genuinely listening. I ask specific questions rather than general ones: 'What's the one thing most slowing you down right now?' is more useful than 'how are things going?' I also ask about things they didn't bring up: 'We haven't talked about the deployment process in a while — is it still a friction point?' I also ask directly about the team, which engineers find easier to answer than questions about themselves: 'Is there anything you think I should know about the team that might not be reaching me?' You're often asking for the thing they're not sure it's their place to say — and explicitly giving them permission to say it changes what you hear."

**"How do you structure a career development conversation?"**

> "I book it separately from the regular 1:1 and I tell the person explicitly what it's for: 'I want to spend an hour understanding what you want from your career so I can make sure the work we're doing together is actually moving you toward it.' I start with the past — what times have you been most energised? what kinds of problems have you most enjoyed? — because that's lower stakes and produces real signal about what they find meaningful. The present is about what's working and what's frustrating right now. The future is where do you want to be in 3 years — and I probe this, because the first answer is often 'staff engineer' or 'leading a team' without the specifics of what that means to them. Once I understand what they actually want, I can design the development intentionally: the right stretch assignments, the right scope of problems, the right feedback focus. Most engineers have never been asked these questions systematically. The conversation itself is usually memorable to them, which is a signal of how rarely it happens."
