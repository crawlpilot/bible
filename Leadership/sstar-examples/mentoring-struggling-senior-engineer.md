# SSTAR: Mentoring a Struggling Senior Engineer to a Breakthrough

**Category**: Leadership · Coaching · Team Development · Principal Engineer Scope  
**Framework**: SSTAR (Situation → Strategy → Task → Action → Result)  
**Interview context**: "Tell me about a time you developed someone on your team" / "Describe a time you helped an engineer who wasn't performing" / "How do you approach coaching senior engineers who are struggling?"

> At PE level, this question tests whether you can grow people — not just deliver projects. Interviewers are evaluating your ability to diagnose root causes of underperformance, tailor your coaching to the individual, and create conditions for lasting growth rather than short-term compliance.

---

## Why Senior Engineer Mentoring is a PE-Level Skill

Mentoring a junior engineer is expected of any senior. At principal level, the harder and more relevant scenario is:
- Mentoring someone at or near your level (senior → staff)
- When the person doesn't know they need coaching
- When the root cause of underperformance is not technical
- When the outcome requires sustained investment over months, not a single conversation

FAANG interviewers are evaluating whether you can be an effective technical lead for a team — and that requires being a developer of people, not just a developer of software.

## SSTAR — Helping a Senior Engineer Transition to Technical Leadership

### S — Situation

*"At [Company], I was the Principal Engineer for our Search Platform. We had a senior engineer named Priya (name changed) who had been with the company for 4 years and was technically excellent — she was the team's deepest expert in our Elasticsearch cluster and had architected our most recent index migration. She was well-liked and respected by peers.*

*Priya had been passed over for a Staff promotion twice in 18 months. Each time, the feedback from the promotion committee was the same: 'excellent technical contribution, but insufficient cross-team influence and project leadership.' She was frustrated but not sure what to do differently — she felt she was doing everything a Staff Engineer was supposed to do.*

*I was asked by the engineering manager to provide technical mentorship to Priya and to be candid in my assessment of what was missing."*

---

### ST — Strategy

*"The first thing I didn't do was tell Priya what the promotion committee had said. Starting with 'here's the feedback from your failed promotion' is a defensive way to receive coaching and doesn't help someone build genuine capability — it teaches them to perform for the committee.*

*My strategy was to observe before advising. I wanted to understand what Priya's actual behavior was — what she was doing and what she was avoiding — rather than starting from the conclusion that she 'needed more cross-team influence.' That framing might be right, but it might also be a proxy for a more specific root cause.*

*I also approached this as a long-term investment. Two promotion misses in 18 months suggested a systemic pattern, not a one-time gap. I was prepared to invest 6–12 months of regular coaching, not a single session."*

---

### T — Task

*"My responsibility was: (1) build a genuine mentoring relationship with Priya, not a feedback-delivery relationship, (2) diagnose the root cause of the promotion gap through observation and structured conversation, (3) design a development plan that would give her real opportunities to build the missing skills, and (4) provide honest coaching — including when her instincts were wrong — without demoralizing her."*

---

### A — Action

**Month 1 — Observation and relationship building:**

*"I started by asking Priya if she'd be open to a biweekly 1:1 focused on career development. I was explicit that I wasn't going to focus on her promotion — I wanted to understand what she found energizing and frustrating about her work, and what she thought the most important technical problems on the team were.*

*In our first 3 sessions, I mostly listened. Priya was analytical, thorough, and deeply knowledgeable about search. But I noticed a pattern: every time she described a cross-team situation — a dependency on another team, a technical decision that needed alignment — she described it as something that 'happened to her' or was someone else's responsibility. She'd say 'we need the Catalog team to fix their indexing latency, but they haven't responded to our tickets.' She never described herself as an agent in cross-team situations.*

*I also attended two of her technical presentations. Both were excellent — technically clear, well-structured. But she stopped at the end without asking for decisions or commitments. She presented findings; she didn't drive conclusions."*

**Month 2–3 — Naming the pattern (carefully):**

*"I raised the pattern in our 4th session. I was careful about how I framed it: not 'you need to be more assertive' (generic and defensive) but 'I've noticed something in how you describe cross-team situations — you seem to position yourself as reacting to other teams rather than shaping their decisions. Is that intentional?'*

*Priya was quiet for a moment, then said: 'I don't want to overstep. They're not my team. I don't think it's my place to tell them what to do.' That was the root cause. Priya believed that cross-team influence required hierarchical authority she didn't have — and as a result, she was systematically withdrawing from exactly the situations where Staff engineers create impact.*

*I told her directly: 'Staff engineers don't have authority over other teams. That's also true. But the job is to create alignment and drive decisions without that authority — that's the skill that separates Staff from Senior. You've been opting out of the most important skill development opportunities because you thought it wasn't your job. It is your job.'*

*She pushed back: 'But if I go to the Catalog team and tell them to change their indexing, why would they listen to me?' I said: 'They listen when you make the case, not when you make the demand. Let's practice that.'"*

**Month 3–5 — Structured practice:**

*"I created two real opportunities for Priya to practice cross-team influence:*

*Opportunity 1: The Catalog indexing latency issue she'd mentioned. Instead of waiting for the Catalog team to respond to a ticket, I coached Priya to write a 1-page problem statement — specific data on how Catalog's indexing latency was affecting Search SLO, estimated user impact, and a specific ask (reduce P99 indexing latency from 800ms to 200ms by Q3). We reviewed it together; I gave feedback on how the framing shifted from 'here's our problem' to 'here's our shared problem and here's what I'm proposing we do about it together.' Priya delivered it directly to the Catalog EM and their tech lead.*

*The response was different from the ticket. The Catalog EM said he hadn't realized the downstream impact. They scheduled a joint working session.*

*Opportunity 2: A cross-team RFC. Our team was proposing a new search index architecture. I asked Priya to lead the RFC process — not just write the design, but run the stakeholder review, solicit input from 4 teams, and facilitate the decision. I attended her RFC presentation as a silent observer. Afterward I gave her written feedback: '3 things you did well, 2 specific things to improve.'*

*What she did well: excellent technical depth, good responses to questions. What she needed to improve: at the end of the RFC, she didn't call for a decision — she said 'let me know if you have questions.' I coached her: 'End every RFC by asking explicitly for approval or stating what happens next if there are no objections. Decisions don't happen by default.'"*

**Month 6 — Promotion cycle:**

*"Priya's manager submitted her for promotion in the next cycle. I wrote her PE review input. I was honest: 'Priya has demonstrated meaningful growth in cross-team influence over the past 6 months. Specifically: [two concrete examples]. I believe the development work is real and will continue. I recommend promotion.'*

*I also coached Priya on how to tell her own story in the promotion packet. She had a tendency to describe her work in passive voice ('the RFC was approved'; 'the Catalog latency issue was resolved'). I worked with her on 3 sessions of active-voice narrative: 'I wrote the problem statement, presented it to the Catalog EM, and facilitated the joint working session that resulted in the latency improvement.'"*

---

### R — Result

*"Priya was promoted to Staff Engineer in that cycle. The promotion committee feedback specifically cited 'demonstrated growth in cross-team influence and driving decisions across organizational boundaries.'*

*In the 12 months following promotion, Priya led 2 cross-team platform initiatives independently — the Catalog indexing partnership became an ongoing quarterly sync she now runs, and she led the search ranking revamp that involved 3 teams and improved click-through rate by 11%.*

*The coaching relationship itself became a template. Our engineering manager asked me to document the 'observe first, name the pattern, structured practice' approach. I ran a 90-minute workshop on technical mentorship for other senior and staff engineers. 3 engineers who attended have reported using the framework with their own reports.*

*Priya now mentors 2 senior engineers on her own. In our last 1:1, she said the biggest shift was understanding that cross-team influence is a skill you practice, not a personality trait you either have or don't."*

## Coaching Notes

| Dimension | PE-Level Signal | Mid-Level Signal |
|-----------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Diagnosis** | Observed behavior for a month before naming the pattern; found the root cause (authority misunderstanding), not just the symptom | Delivered the promotion committee feedback in the first session |
| **Framing** | "I've noticed a pattern — is this intentional?" (curious) | "You need to improve cross-team influence" (directive) |
| **Development method** | Real opportunities (Catalog problem, RFC) with debrief; not role-play exercises | Reading list + advice sessions |
| **Honesty** | Told her directly that she was opting out of the most important skill development; coached her on her own promotion narrative | Soft-pedaled the difficult feedback |
| **Systemic leverage** | Documented the approach; ran workshop; 3 others adopted it | Helped Priya; did not scale the learning |

## Common Follow-up Questions

**"How do you handle a situation where someone isn't open to coaching?"**
> "I've found that resistance to coaching usually has a cause: they don't trust the coach, they think the feedback is wrong, or they're protecting against past negative experiences. I start by asking — 'what would make this kind of conversation useful for you?' and let them shape the structure. If someone is consistently uninterested in growth after good-faith effort, I shift from active coaching to honest feedback: I tell them directly what I observe and what I think the career implications are, and let them decide. I don't continue investing heavily in someone who doesn't want it."

**"What's different about coaching a senior vs. a junior engineer?"**
> "Junior engineers usually need technical foundations and confidence. Senior engineers usually need to understand that the next level requires a different kind of work — the behaviors that got them to Senior are not the behaviors that get them to Staff. The hardest part of coaching seniors is that they're competent and often confident, which means they may not see the gap. The most effective approach I've found is to make the gap visible through concrete examples rather than abstract feedback."

**"How do you balance your own engineering work with mentoring investment?"**
> "I treat mentoring as a multiplier, not a cost. The 3 hours I spent coaching Priya per month produced a Staff engineer who is now independently leading cross-team initiatives that I don't need to be involved in. That's a better return than 3 hours of my own coding. But it requires prioritizing the investment explicitly — it doesn't happen incidentally alongside other work."
